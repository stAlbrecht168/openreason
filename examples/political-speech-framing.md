# Example input: political speech framing — framework complementarity

## Purpose of this example

This example demonstrates how the four frameworks in the Framing and Rhetoric Pack complement each other. The same input is analysed through Entman, Lakoff, Aristotle, and Perelman in sequence, showing what each framework contributes that the others cannot provide.

---

## Statement to analyse

> "Our nation faces a crisis of dependency. Hardworking families are bearing the burden of those who choose not to contribute. We must restore the dignity of work and protect the taxpayers who built this country. Real reform means giving people the tools to stand on their own two feet, not trapping them in a cycle of handouts."

## Context

This type of statement appears in political speeches, budget debates, and welfare policy discussions. The speaker is unspecified. The statement addresses welfare and social support policy.

## What each framework contributes

### Entman — structural framing (what the frame says)

Entman maps the four-part structure of the frame.

- **Problem definition:** Dependency — framed as a social crisis caused by recipients choosing not to contribute.
- **Causal interpretation:** Individual choice ("those who choose not to contribute") rather than structural factors (unemployment, disability, care responsibilities).
- **Moral evaluation:** Hardworking taxpayers are virtuous; those receiving support are a burden, implicitly lacking dignity.
- **Treatment recommendation:** Reform that gives people "tools to stand on their own" — i.e., reducing or conditioning support.
- **Omissions:** The frame omits structural causes of poverty, the role of unpaid care work, disability, labour market conditions, and the economic multiplier effects of social support spending.

Use Entman when you need to describe what the frame asserts and what it forecloses.

### Lakoff — cognitive framing (why the frame works)

Lakoff explains the cognitive mechanism underneath the Entman frame.

- **Conceptual metaphor:** "Dependency" activates an addiction/substance metaphor — dependency is harmful, self-limiting, and requires breaking. "Burden" activates a weight metaphor — taxpayers carry those who cannot carry themselves.
- **Frame activation:** "Hardworking families" activates the strict father moral model: those who work hard deserve what they earn; those who do not work hard are freeloaders. This moral model is packed into the adjective "hardworking," not stated explicitly.
- **"Stand on their own two feet"** — the self-sufficiency metaphor frames independence as physical capability. Dependence on support becomes equivalent to physical inability to stand. The frame hides that "standing" in a market economy requires conditions (jobs, affordable housing, childcare) that are collectively produced.
- **Reframing risk:** A counterargument that says "dependency is actually caused by structural factors" uses the word "dependency," reinforcing the dependency frame even while arguing against it. A reframe would need to offer an alternative conceptual structure ("investment in human capability" or "foundations for participation").

Use Lakoff when you need to explain *why* the frame is persuasive and *how* rebuttal is constrained.

### Aristotle — rhetorical appeals (how the audience is moved)

Aristotle identifies the three modes of appeal and the enthymeme.

- **Ethos:** "Those who built this country" constructs the taxpayer as the legitimate inheritor of the nation — someone whose claim to speak on national interests is earned through contribution. The speaker aligns with this figure.
- **Pathos:** The emotional register is resentment and solidarity — resentment at "those who choose not to contribute," solidarity with "hardworking families." Resentment requires a perceived injustice (the hardworking paying for the non-contributing); solidarity requires a shared identity (taxpayers vs. dependents).
- **Logos / enthymeme:** The argument has an unstated premise: that people who receive support do so by choice rather than necessity. The audience must supply this premise for the argument to work. If the audience does not supply it, the argument fails to land. Identifying the enthymeme reveals the ideological assumption the text requires.

Use Aristotle when you need to identify which appeal mode is in use and what the audience must assume.

### Perelman — argumentation schemes (what type of argument this is)

Perelman identifies the specific scheme types and value hierarchies.

- **Quasi-logical argument (justice):** "Bearing the burden of those who choose not to contribute" invokes a justice principle (equal contribution for equal benefit). This is structured to appear mathematically fair, borrowing the prestige of formal equality — but it applies a procedural equality principle to circumstances that are not equal.
- **Dissociation (dignity):** "Restore the dignity of work" performs a dissociation: true dignity (valued) is associated with work; the implied counterpart — dignity without work, or dignity as a condition of receiving support — is devalued. This means any defence of support as dignified can be categorised as the devalued version.
- **Dissociation (reform):** "Real reform" dissociates authentic reform (tools to stand independently) from inauthentic reform (continuing support). The dissociation insulates the speaker's preferred policy from critique by making alternatives definitionally not "real" reform.
- **Value hierarchy:** The argument establishes a hierarchy: earned contribution > unearned receipt; work > support; individual capability > collective provision.

Use Perelman when you need to identify the specific argument scheme type and the dissociations that protect the argument from counterargument.

## What each framework uniquely provides

| Framework | Unique contribution | Evidence statuses used |
|---|---|---|
| **Entman** | Structural four-part map: what is the problem, cause, moral evaluation, solution? What is omitted? | F1, S1 |
| **Lakoff** | Cognitive mechanism: which conceptual metaphors and moral models are activated? Why does rebuttal reinforce? | F1, C1 |
| **Aristotle** | Appeal modes: ethos/pathos/logos, emotional register, enthymeme (what must audience supply?) | R1, L1 |
| **Perelman** | Argument scheme type: quasi-logical? analogy? dissociation? What value hierarchy is assumed or established? | R1, F1, L1 |

All four together produce an analysis that explains: what is claimed (Entman), why it works cognitively (Lakoff), how the audience is moved (Aristotle), and what type of argument makes the claim seem reasonable (Perelman).

---

## Complementarity, not redundancy

These frameworks produce different findings on the same text — they are not saying the same thing in different vocabularies.

- Entman tells you the frame omits structural causes of poverty. Lakoff tells you the word "dependency" makes structural-cause arguments harder to make, because the dependency frame is already active.
- Aristotle tells you the emotional register is resentment-and-solidarity. Perelman tells you the argument structure that makes this resentment seem fair (quasi-logical justice principle).
- Aristotle identifies the enthymeme (the audience must assume recipients choose not to work). Perelman explains how dissociation ("dignity of work") insulates this assumption from challenge.

## Out of scope

- Whether the specific claims (burden, choice, crisis) are empirically accurate
- The speaker's political affiliation or intent
- Whether the described policy would achieve the stated goals
